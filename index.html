About Me

I was born and raised in East Tennessee, where I formed a lifelong love of the outdoors, wildlife, and nature. I received my B.S. in Wildlife and Fisheries Sciences with a concentration in Wildlife Health from the University of Tennessee, Knoxville. There, I completed undergraduate research studying microplastics contaminant concentrations in threatened little brown bats, which you can read more about here. My personal values and interests, as well as my academic accomplishments and professional goals, led me to pursue a Masters in Biology at Austin Peay State University in Clarksville, Tennessee, where I study bats, hibernation energetics, and fungal disease in Dr. Catherine Haase's mammalogy lab , which you can read more about here.

Career goals

Upon graduating with my Master's in Biology, I intend on finding a career in science education, either on an intermediate-school, community-college, or non-profit scale. I am passionate about getting more girls and women into the sciences and aim to have a career that allows me to help do that. I am also interested in helping adult students reach their academic goals in biology, ecology, statistics, and other fields. I enjoy doing research and have found a great love for teaching; I hope to have a career that allows me to continue to do both.

Research goals

Past Research

As an undergraduate student at the University of Tennessee, Knoxville, I conducted research in Dr. Emma Willcox's Lab. I spent multiple years working for PhD candidate Ash Cable as both a field technician during summer field season months and as a laboratory technician during non-field season months. As a field technician, I captured, identfied, and collected morphometric information, as well as hair and guano samples, from hundreds of bats as a part of Ash's doctorate dissertation. As a laboratory technician, I collected lung, liver, kidney, and gastrointestinal tract samples from deceased bats during necropsy and performed chemical digestion of these samples in order for us to analyze microplastic concentrations. Inspired by this part of Ash's dissertation, I designed my own study, using organs I collected from little brown bats (Myotis lucifugus) that died in the early 2000's as a result of white-nose syndrome. The published manuscript for this research that I completed can be found here.

Microplastics Exposure in At-Risk Myotis lucifugus Bats of the Northeastern United States

Abstract

This project compares measured microplastics concentrations from stomach contents in Myotis lucifugus (M. lucifugus) bats from natural and manmade caves in the northeastern United States. Samples used in this study were collected from caves in Vermont and from mines in Massachusetts and New York from 2008- 2009. Stomach and intestine contents from 92 bats were chemically digested, filtered, and observed; microplastics from each sample were classified based on type and color. A subset of microplastic samples was measured to obtain data on average microplastic size. The objectives of this study are (1) to quantify microplastic concentrations from M. lucifugus bats; (2) to compare microplastic concentrations between male and female M. lucifugus; and (3) to compare microplastic concentrations between juvenile and adult M. lucifugus. Results from this project suggest that while microplastics are present M. lucifugus habitats, no significant difference exists in microplastic concentrations of the stomach contents of male versus female M. lucifugus. Therefore, it is difficult to infer how this may impact M. lucifugus reproduction and/or conservation. This study should be used in conjunction with others to evaluate the role that microplastics play in bats‚Äô response to stressors such as white nose syndrome, habitat destruction/alteration, and other environmental contaminants to assess potential related conservation and reproduction-based consequences.

Current Research

USFWS-FUNDED RESEARCH GRANT

I was hired to conduct research with the Texas Parks and Wildlife Department for a grant funded by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. This project aims to determine the consequences of tri-colored bat (Perimyotis subflavus) hibernation behavior on white-nose syndrome (WNS) pathogen (Pseudogymnoascus destructans) dynamics in non-traditional hibernacula. The work I am completing for this grant involves field work in under-highway culverts in eastern Texas, where several populations of P. subflavus hibernate, including PIT tagging several hundred P. subflavus throughout hibernation to analyze roost fidelity, using temperature-sensitive radio telemetry equipment to track bat torpor bouts and arousals over hibernation periods, and collecting microclimate data from these culverts.

Within the framework of conservation physiology, this project aims to answer these questions:

  • What are the thermoregulatory strategies of culvert-roosting bats in Texas?
  • What are the roosting behaviors, including roost fidelity and roost switching, of Texas tri-colored bats?
  • What are the micrclimate characteristics of Texas culverts?
  • Do the hibernation behaviors of culvert-roosting bats, combined with variable microclimates, slow WNS?

GRADUATE THESIS RESEARCH

As a graduate student at Austin Peay State University, I am a member of the Haase Mammalogy Lab, where I study bat hibernation and fungal disease. While the larger USFWS project focuses on interactions between the host, pathogen, and environment of WNS cases, my thesis project focuses on the latter two. I am collaborating with researchers in Texas, Oklahoma, Tennessee, and South Carolina to gather tri-colored bat hibernacula microclimate data to study how some P. subflavus populations in the southeastern United States evade the fungal disease, despite the presence of its pathogen, P. destructans in these hibernacula.

I hypothesize that microclimate, specifically temperature and water vapor pressure, drives the probability of WNS in tri-colored bat hibernacula in the Southeastern United States.

  • Sites with WNS will have warmer daily and monthly temperature, lower daily and monthly humidity, and therefore will spend a smaller portion of the winter within the optimal growth range of P. destructans.
  • The probability of WNS occurring in a hibernaculum is best explained by temperatrue and water vapor pressure variability, not latitude, elevation, site type, or other variables.
